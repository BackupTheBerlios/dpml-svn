The Metro Community Building Style

Open Source Software (OSS) in various shapes and forms, is all about "Open the Source Code to Everyone". We want to bring the OSS concept to the next level.

Invite Everyone

Organizations like Apache Software Foundation, claims to have a Bazar of activities where everyone is jolly happy and having fun, creating code and programs to the better of human kind. The result of these activities, we all know, are genuinely good. Noone can question that. But is it necessarily the best process to go about it? We think we have identified a problem in the ASF model, and hope to show that there are other ways, and we are willing to lead the way.

At ASF, it is fun! if you are on the INSIDE. To get inside is not all that easy, but once inside you will remain. Pretty much forever, unless you resign. What does that mean? I struggle like hell, to show I am worthy and get the approval of the others on the inside, that I should be part of the fun. Once inside, I can relax, be opinion maker, make decisions, and contribute to the codebase at my leisure, if at all.

I think there is something basically wrong with this model. It is not an Open Participation model. Those who are actively working on the code, docs, PR materials, participating in the daily chores of the community and have an updated perspective of the issues at hand, should be part of the decision process. Furthermore, if I have spent the time to create a patch, I AM committed, no doubt.

- "Hi! I have created this patch that ..."

- "Jolly Good, ol' chap. Why don't you commit it into the codebase yourself?"

That means, take away the concept of commit rights. You want to join, join!. Be my guest. We are looking at the principle of Wiki applied to the code scenario. If Wiki works, why wouldn't this?

Open Participation Software

Wiki and the Open Participation Software (OPS) builds on the metaphor of "A Flock of Birds".

No individual bird in the flock sets the direction of the flock. They all sets the direction of the flock, driven by instincts of the moment in each individual. Any bird leaving the flock, is no longer a PartOf the flock and the collective decision process.

Brought on to our scene, it means that everyone that is currently part of the community, adds to the collective decision making. If I am on vacation and not staying in touch with daily issues, I am not part of the flock. When I come back from my vacation, I rejoin as a single small part of the collective mind.

Could this possibly work? Could one make a code repository open to everyone who has an interest? Wouldn't everything just deteriorate rapidly into a 'nothing'?

Interesting questions, and the same questions were raised against the Wiki concept. It has, IMO, proved the dooms-sayers wrong. It works, because everyone wants it to work. Everyone benefits, everyone enjoys the result.

But what about licensing? You can't license the code without being the owner, and for that you need Copyright assigns, indemnification agreements, ...

Do we really need a License (BSD, Apache style) that basically says "Do whatever you want with this code, as long as you don't remove the Copyrights and don't use our name(s) to promote your derived work." ? IMHO, this is a matter of Pride. If I am truly "Open", can't I just say; "Here is a bit of code, do what you like with it."? Why do I need to add, the ", BUT...".

And if one sets out with that in mind, there is actually a legal construct for this, called the Public Domain. Scientists has used it for centuries, why wouldn't it work for us? Each individual who wants to commit, needs to express the desire of no claim of Copyright. I think that can be done through the registration process, otherwise we need to require some template message saying that in the commit message for it to be accepted into the repository. This needs some legal advice.

Open Participation in Practice

There do exist a couple of threats to this model, but IMO the oportunities far outweighs these threats.

Best Practices

As far as I know, this is new, so there are no best practices yet. We need to make it work, since we want it to work, and that means willingness to adapt to the situations that will occur. However, drawing from the ASF experience I think there are some things we should consider;

Communication

People is a communicative species. It is engrain in our very existence the need to communicate, unlike for instance apes that 'groom' each other as mean of social networking. Mailing lists has many weaknesses, most importantly it has too long of feedback loop, so each post gets 'too loaded' with information and may trigger so called flame fests. I strongly believe in IRC and IM as the primary tool for communication of intent, discussions and suggestions, and the mailing list only for questions and traceability of progress.

Code Hierarchy

It will become impossible for everyone to be part of everything, and many things, perhaps most things, doesn't even affect me. So if the codebase has some form of hierarchy, that is reasonably easy to understand, each individual will have different levels of interest in each leaf node, and should then monitor any changes in the codebase in the branches leading to these nodes, and take on an active review role to make sure the changes looks good. By such recommendation, more and more people will be responsible the nearer the top you get, which should ensure higher level of quality, minimizing risks of breaking compatibility, correct level of functionality and probably many other aspects of evolution.

Code Ladder

Orthogonally to the hierarchy, which will probably be a notion and not a rule, I think it is reasonable to have some form of code ladder. Any new piece of code starts in experimental level where the piece is developed initially by one or many people, and when it is operational is promoted to preliminary level where others can actually use it. Then I think that if it attracts enough flock and they can iron out any issues, it is promoted into a optional level and becomes part of the recommended set of options to the average user. Once it is tried on the field, it may be moved up to the stable level, sending the signal to the users that there are no known issues. Finally, certain pieces could enter frozen meaning that no changes will be made, either due to an API that can't be changed or that some other system has taken its role. The question is, would the code ladder have formal requirements of development, which would require a lot of tool support, or is the flock a guarantor enough to ensure the recommendation is followed. I think the latter is sufficient.

Spammers, Vandalism and Saboteurs

I am talking about the similarity of Wiki spam and such, not determined attacks to shut us down. I call it spam and spammers from now on.

I think there are a couple of mechanisms that can prevent this. First of all, compared to Wiki, one needs quite a bit more knowledge in development to have subversion installed and know how to operate it against our repository.

Secondly, we have a registration system, so that we at least have a valid email address after the fact. Not much but adds a little bit.

Thirdly, we need a good notification and archiving system. I have in mind that all commits, irregardless of size, are notified to everyone, and stored in a mail archive.

Fourthly, we probably need a commandline utility which you give a svn revision number and it removes the change made from the previous revision to that one. Effectively, make it easier to revert the change than make the change.

Troublemakers

Troublemaker is someone who is highly active in the community, but doesn't have the collective trust to be part of the collective decision making process.

My take on this at the moment is that if the "Cost of Change" is higher than the "Cost of Reversal", the collective has an extreme advantage, in that not only is it less of trouble for the individual who makes the reversal, but also the scale factor of the flock. Meaning, if a trouble making individual is trying to commit changes against the collective, he/she will soon give up, since it requires too much energy to fight the flock. Also, if the individual is gathering supporters, i.e. his/her own flock, then just point out the procedure of going through the Code Ladder and allow the fork. If it is good, the main flock will replace it when the change has matured.
